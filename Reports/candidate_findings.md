## Background

This analysis examines Medicare Part D diabetes GLP-1 prescribing for 2022 and 2023, covering four brands (Ozempic, Trulicity, Mounjaro, Rybelsus) across 169,404 unique prescribers and $18.3B in annual spend. Three candidate findings emerge from year-over-year share-shift analysis, decile segmentation, and specialty-level breakdowns. Each finding is documented with supporting evidence and the specific risks that need to be tested before final inclusion in the strategic deck.

---

## Finding 1: Novo's 2023 share gain came from Trulicity's collapse, not from beating Mounjaro

**Claim:** Contrary to the prevailing public narrative that Lilly is winning the GLP-1 category, Novo Nordisk gained 8.1 percentage points of Medicare diabetes GLP-1 share in 2023. The gain was driven by absorbing Trulicity refugees rather than by competing successfully against Mounjaro.

**Evidence:**
- Trulicity collapsed from 54.6% share in 2022 to 35.8% in 2023 — a loss of 18.8 percentage points in a single year
- Mounjaro grew from 0.5% to 11.2% — capturing 10.7pp, less than what Trulicity lost
- Ozempic absorbed the remaining 8 percentage points, growing from 38.9% to 47.4%
- At company level: Novo moved from minority position (44.9%) to majority (53.0%); Lilly fell from 55.1% to 47.0%
- Mounjaro's prescriber count grew 21x year-over-year (2,034 prescribers in 2022 → 44,382 in 2023)

**Strategic implication:** Novo's macro share lead is real but built on a competitor's product transition rather than competitive victory. As Trulicity's decline tapers and Mounjaro's adoption matures, the dynamics will likely reverse. Novo's strategic priority should not be celebrating the 2023 gain but consolidating it before Mounjaro reaches full clinical uptake.

**Risk to the claim:** This finding is structurally sound — the math reconciles at brand, company, and decile levels. However, the framing depends on attributing causality to Trulicity's collapse. The risk is that some portion of Ozempic's gain represents genuine competitive wins against Mounjaro that I'm misclassifying as Trulicity-conversion. Validation requires examining prescriber-level switching patterns: of doctors who reduced Trulicity in 2023, what % shifted to Ozempic vs. Mounjaro vs. Rybelsus? If most went to Ozempic, the finding holds. If they split evenly, the story is more nuanced.

## Finding 2: Mounjaro is concentrated in the top decile — where Lilly's structural threat to Novo is real

**Claim:** While Novo's macro share lead appears decisive, the underlying decile structure reveals a vulnerability: Mounjaro's penetration triples from the bottom to the top of the prescriber pyramid, peaking at 14.2% in Decile 10 — the segment that drives 42% of all category volume. Within endocrinology specifically, this concentration is even more pronounced.

**Evidence:**
- Mounjaro share by decile: Decile 1: 6.6%, Decile 3: 4.5%, Decile 7: 8.6%, Decile 10: 14.2%
- Decile 10 (top 10% of prescribers, ~16,932 doctors) writes 42.1% of all GLP-1 claims
- Ozempic's share is the inverse: highest in Deciles 2-3 (55%) and lowest in Decile 10 (45.5%)
- Within endocrinology, top-decile prescribers average 1,036 claims/year (one prescriber wrote 6,704)
- Endocrinology represents only 3.4% of prescribers but 14% of total volume — heavy over-indexing in Decile 10
- At the company level, Lilly's Decile 10 share is 48.8% in 2023 (vs Novo's 51.2%) — essentially tied, the only decile where this is true

**Strategic implication:** Novo's franchise is structurally weakest where it matters most. The 5,700 endocrinologists in the dataset, particularly the ~570 in their within-specialty top decile, are where Mounjaro's efficacy advantage is most influential and where competitive losses would be most damaging. Defensive investment should be asymmetrically concentrated here: increased rep frequency, head-to-head clinical evidence generation, KOL programs, cardiovascular outcomes messaging that Mounjaro cannot yet match.

**Risk to the claim:** The 14.2% Decile 10 share is meaningful but not yet dominant — Ozempic still leads. The strategic urgency depends on the *trajectory* of Mounjaro's top-decile adoption. If 2022→2023 already showed Mounjaro overtaking incumbents in Decile 10 specifically, the finding is strong. If Mounjaro's Decile 10 share grew at the same rate as overall share, the "structural threat" framing may be overstated. Validation requires building the 2022 brand-by-decile table and computing year-over-year change within each decile, especially Decile 10.

## Finding 3: The market is three commercially distinct segments, not one — requiring three different strategies

**Claim:** Within-specialty decile analysis reveals that the Medicare diabetes GLP-1 market is not a single homogeneous market but three structurally different ones: High-Intensity Specialists, Primary Care, and Mid-Level Providers. Each segment has different volume distributions, different prescribing behaviors, and different competitive dynamics — meaning a uniform commercial strategy will systematically underperform a segmented one.

**Evidence:**
- High-Intensity Specialists (Endocrinology): ~5,700 prescribers; top decile averages 1,036 claims/year; max prescriber wrote 6,704
- Primary Care (Family Practice + Internal Medicine combined): ~95,000 prescribers; top decile averages 240-315 claims/year
- Mid-Level Providers (Nurse Practitioners + Physician Assistants combined): ~56,000 prescribers; top decile averages 270-280 claims/year
- A bottom-decile endocrinologist (avg 26 claims) prescribes more than a fifth-decile family practice doctor (avg 42 claims) — the segments don't overlap meaningfully on volume
- Each segment has distinct competitive dynamics: Mounjaro's penetration is most pronounced among specialists; Ozempic's strongest position is in mid-deciles of primary care

**Strategic implication:** Novo should explicitly design three different commercial plays. For specialists: clinical evidence, KOL relationships, congress presence, head-to-head data. For primary care: brand familiarity, rep coverage, samples, simple efficacy messaging. For mid-level providers (the segment most pharma companies under-invest in): dedicated coverage with content tuned to NP/PA workflows. Treating these segments uniformly with one rep model and one message will yield suboptimal results across all three.

**Risk to the claim:** The three-segment framework is logical and supported by the volume distributions, but it may be too clean. Real prescribers fall on a continuum, and some heavy-volume Family Practice doctors may behave more like specialists, while some lower-volume endocrinologists may behave more like generalists. Validation requires testing whether *within-segment* variance in Mounjaro-vs-Ozempic share is meaningfully smaller than *between-segment* variance. If yes, the segmentation is real. If within-segment variance is high, the segmentation may need to be refined or abandoned.

## Methodology Caveats

- All findings reflect Medicare Part D diabetes-indicated prescribing only. Public discourse on the GLP-1 category typically focuses on the obesity segment (Wegovy vs Zepbound), where competitive dynamics differ substantially. Findings should not be extrapolated to commercial payers, the obesity market, or international markets.
- Medicare Part D captures approximately 30% of total US GLP-1 prescribing volume; directional findings should hold but absolute share figures will differ from market-wide views.
- CMS suppresses prescriber-drug records with ≤10 unique patients, biasing analysis toward higher-volume prescribers (minimum observed claims = 11). Bottom-decile findings should be interpreted accordingly.
- 2022 vs 2023 represents a single year-over-year comparison; multi-year trajectory data would strengthen confidence in directional findings and is recommended for Phase 2.

### Test 4 — Senior Partner Objection

**Three-word objection:** "Coincidence, not cause."

**Full objection:**

"You're claiming Trulicity refugees went to Ozempic specifically. But you don't have prescriber-level switching data. All you have is two snapshots — 2022 and 2023 share. The math reconciles, sure, but maybe Trulicity patients went to ALL the alternatives roughly proportionally to their existing market share, including Mounjaro. Or maybe new GLP-1 prescribers entered the market in 2023 and disproportionately chose Ozempic — meaning Novo's gain is from greenfield, not from Trulicity at all. You're inferring causation from aggregate share movements. That's a stretch."

**Rebuttal**

"The objection is fair on its face — Medicare prescriber-drug data is aggregated, so I can't observe individual patient switches directly. However, the prescriber-count data supports the Trulicity-conversion thesis specifically:
- Trulicity prescribers grew from ~109K to ~110K (essentially flat) — meaning very few NEW doctors started prescribing Trulicity in 2023
- Ozempic prescribers grew from ~72K to ~132K (+60K, +83%)
- Mounjaro prescribers grew from ~2K to ~44K (+42K)
- Total prescriber universe grew from 124K to 169K (+45K, +36%)
If 'random redistribution proportional to existing share' were the explanation, we'd expect new prescribers to choose all four brands roughly in proportion to their 2022 shares (Trulicity 55%, Ozempic 39%, Rybelsus 6%, Mounjaro 0.5%). Instead, almost no new prescribers chose Trulicity, while Ozempic and Mounjaro absorbed the entire growth. That pattern is directly inconsistent with random redistribution and consistent with active substitution away from Trulicity.
On the greenfield-vs-substitution objection: even if some of Ozempic's growth came from new prescribers (greenfield), the company-level math — Novo +8.1pp, Lilly -8.1pp — necessarily means Ozempic absorbed share from Lilly's prior position. The reconciliation isn't decorative. It's accounting.
Verdict: the objection identifies a real data limitation, but the inference holds because the prescriber-count pattern provides triangulating evidence."

"The objection is partially right and partially missing the point. Yes, top-decile prescribers do try new drugs first — that's genuinely how diffusion works. It's 'in Decile 10 specifically, only 23% of Trulicity's lost share went to Ozempic, while 80% went to Mounjaro — whereas in Decile 3, Ozempic captured 86%.' That's not a story about adoption speed. It's a story about competitive substitution: when a top-decile doctor lost their Trulicity, they chose differently than a mid-decile doctor did.
If the objection were correct — that this is just diffusion timing — Mounjaro would be slightly elevated in the top decile but Ozempic would still dominate the share-shift. Instead, in Decile 10, Mounjaro outright won the share-shift contest 13.4pp to 3.8pp. That's a 3.5x dominance ratio, not a 1.2x 'top-decile bonus.'
Furthermore, the same pattern was visible in 2022, when Mounjaro held only 0.5% overall share but was already 4-8x more concentrated in Decile 10 than in lower deciles. If this were generic 'top-decile try-everything' behavior, all drugs would show the same elevation. Trulicity, by contrast, was nearly flat across deciles in 2022 (51-62%) — the canonical pattern of a mature, fully-diffused drug. Mounjaro's pattern is specifically different from Trulicity's pattern, and that difference is the story.
Verdict: the objection is right that top-decile doctors adopt new drugs faster, but the share-shift competition between Ozempic and Mounjaro in Decile 10 is not explained by general adoption dynamics. It reflects specific competitive choice among displaced Trulicity prescribers, where Mounjaro won."


**Three-word objection:** "Just specialty differences."

**Full objection:**

"You haven't segmented anything. You've labeled the obvious — specialists prescribe more, generalists prescribe less, NPs and PAs sit in between. Every commercial pharma team in the country already organizes their sales force by specialty. Calling this 'three commercially distinct segments' isn't an insight; it's a restatement of how pharma sales forces have been structured for 30 years. Where's the new strategic information?"

**Rebuttal**

"The objection lands part of the way. Specialty-based segmentation is indeed standard pharma structure, and labeling specialties as 'segments' adds limited new value on its own. The genuine contribution of Finding 3 is two specific things the standard specialty-based view misses:
First, the volume-distribution differences are larger than commonly assumed. A bottom-decile endocrinologist (avg 26 claims) prescribes more than a fifth-decile family practice doctor (avg 42). The within-segment variance and between-segment variance overlap less than the conventional view suggests. This means uniform 'specialty rep coverage' (one endo rep, one PCP rep) under-prioritizes the top deciles within each specialty — a within-specialty decile-based call plan would outperform a uniform specialty-based plan.

Endocrinology supply is structurally fixed by specialty training pipelines. This changes the strategic implication: defending share among endos is defending a non-replenishing customer base. Defending share among primary care or NPs is defending one wave of a continuously growing market. These are not equivalent strategic positions, and the standard 'specialty-based field force' framing doesn't capture this distinction.
Verdict: the objection is correct that specialty-based segmentation is well-known. The actual contribution of Finding 3 is (a) within-specialty volume tiering and (b) the fixed-supply nature of endocrinology — both of which sharpen the standard specialty view rather than replicate it."

## Why-Drill Conclusion (Finding 2): The Pattern Is Volume-Driven, But Geography Determines Where the Battle Is Most Acute

After Finding 2 emerged from pressure testing, the natural next question was: **why** does the split-pyramid pattern exist? Why do top-decile prescribers favor Mounjaro while lower-decile prescribers favor Ozempic? Three candidate hypotheses were considered:

- **Hypothesis A — Clinical Evidence:** Top-decile prescribers (especially endocrinologists) read trial data and choose Mounjaro on scientific merit
- **Hypothesis B — Rep Coverage:** Lilly's sales force concentrates on top-decile doctors more aggressively than on lower-decile doctors
- **Hypothesis C — Patient Complexity:** Top-decile prescribers handle harder cases that benefit from Mounjaro's higher-potency molecule

Hypothesis A was tested first because it was the most strategically meaningful and the most directly testable with the available data.

### Test 1 — Specialty Hypothesis (REJECTED)

If clinical evidence is the driver, the split-pyramid pattern should be concentrated specifically in endocrinology — the specialty most likely to make evidence-based decisions. The diagnostic was simple: examine Novo's share within each (specialty × decile) cell across the five major prescribing specialties.

**Predicted output if Hypothesis A is correct:** Novo's Decile 10 share should be substantially lower in endocrinology (40% or below) than in primary care or other specialties (52-55%). The endocrinology column should look visibly different from the others.

**The Result:** The Decile 10 row of the (specialty × decile) Novo-share grid is essentially flat across all five specialties:

| Specialty | Decile 10 Novo Share |
|---|---|
| Nurse Practitioner | 49.8% |
| Family Practice | 50.7% |
| Endocrinology | **51.0%** |
| Physician Assistant | 51.5% |
| Internal Medicine | 52.4% |

Range: 2.6 percentage points. Endocrinology is not special. Lilly's strength at the top decile is uniform across all specialties — primary care, mid-level providers, and specialists alike. The clinical-evidence-driven specialty story does not explain the split-pyramid pattern.

### What Specialty Failed to Explain, Geography Reveals

While the specialty test refuted Hypothesis A, it pointed to a more universal pattern: every specialty showed the same volume-driven dynamic. Within each specialty, low-decile prescribers strongly favored Novo (55-71%) while high-decile prescribers were contested (49-54%).

To understand *where* this volume-driven dynamic is intensifying fastest, the analysis was extended to a geographic cut. The result reveals significant heterogeneity that the national-level data was hiding.

The SURPASS-2 head-to-head trial showed greater A1C reduction (2.0% vs 1.4%) and greater weight loss (12kg vs 6kg). For the harder-to-control patients who concentrate in high-volume practices, this clinical difference is meaningful.

### What to Do

**Headline action:** Reallocate Novo's commercial investment from broad market coverage toward concentrated defensive engagement of the top-decile prescriber population — approximately 17,000 doctors writing 42% of Medicare diabetes GLP-1 volume.

The reallocation has three specific components:

**1. Increase rep call frequency on top-decile prescribers from current ~6-8 visits per year to ~12-15 visits per year.** Source the additional capacity by reducing rep coverage of Decile 1-3 prescribers (~52,000 prescribers writing only 6% of category volume — structurally low ROI per rep visit). Move those low-volume prescribers to digital and educational channels.

**2. Concentrate medical affairs and clinical evidence investment on cardiovascular outcomes positioning.** Ozempic's SELECT trial demonstrated reduced cardiovascular events in patients with diabetes and obesity — a clinical benefit Mounjaro has not yet established. Cardiovascular outcomes is Novo's defensible clinical moat in the top decile, where prescribers are most attentive to outcome differentiation. Pair this with KOL development programs targeting top-decile endocrinologists and high-volume primary care diabetes leaders.

**3. Restructure Novo's field force from specialty-based to volume-based call planning.** The why-drill analysis demonstrated that top-decile primary care doctors behave commercially like top-decile endocrinologists — both contested, both favoring Mounjaro at similar rates. A specialty-based field force misallocates effort by under-prioritizing high-volume primary care providers and over-prioritizing low-volume specialists. The new model should treat decile rank as the primary segmentation variable for high-volume prescribers, with specialty as a secondary attribute.
